    Section 2.4 The Limit Laws

    Learning Objectives.

    1. Recognize the basic limit laws.
      🔗
    2. Use the limit laws to evaluate the limit of a function.
      🔗
    3. Use the limit laws to evaluate the limit of a polynomial or rational function.
      🔗
    4. Evaluate the limit of a function by factoring or by using conjugates.
      🔗
    5. Evaluate the limit of a function by using the squeeze theorem.
      🔗
    In Section 2.2, we evaluated limits by looking at graphs or by constructing a table of values. In this section, we establish laws for calculating limits and learn how to apply these laws.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.1 The Limit Laws

    A few basic rules allow us to break down complicated functions into simpler ones when calculating limits. By using these limit laws, we can simplify many limit computations. We start with two important limits.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.1. Basic Limit Results.

    For any real number \(a\) and any constant \(c\text{,}\)
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \limit_{x \to a }x=a\)
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \limit_{x \to a }c=c\)
      🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    1. For \(\varepsilon \gt 0\text{,}\) let \(\delta=\varepsilon\text{.}\) Whenever
      \begin{equation*} 0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta \end{equation*}
      then
      \begin{equation*} |f(x)-a|=|x-a|\lt\delta=\varepsilon \end{equation*}
      Therefore, \(\ds\lim_{x\to a}x=a\)
      🔗
      🔗
    2. For \(\varepsilon \gt 0\text{,}\) let \(\delta=\varepsilon\text{.}\) Whenever
      \begin{equation*} 0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta \end{equation*}
      then
      \begin{equation*} |f(x)-c|=|c-c|=0\lt\delta=\varepsilon \end{equation*}
      Therefore, \(\ds\lim_{x\to a}c=c\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.2. Evaluating a Basic Limit.

    Evaluate each of the following limits using Theorem 2.4.1.
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \limit_{x \to 2 }x\)
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \limit_{x \to 2 }5\)
      🔗
    Solution.
    1. The limit of \(x\) as \(x\) approaches \(a\) is \(a\text{:}\) \(\limit_{x \to 2 }x=2.\)
      🔗
    2. The limit of a constant is that constant: \(\limit_{x \to 2 }5=5.\)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    We now take a look at the limit laws, the individual properties of limits. The proofs that these laws hold are included at the end of the section.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.3. Limit Laws.

    Let \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) be defined for all \(x\neq a\) over some open interval containing \(a\text{.}\) Assume that \(L \) and \(M \) are real numbers such that \(\limit_{x \to a }f(x)=L\) and \(\limit_{x \to a }g(x)=M.\) Let \(c\) be a constant. Then, each of the following statements holds:
    🔗
    Sum law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }(f(x)+g(x))=\limit_{x \to a }f(x)+\limit_{x \to a }g(x)=L+M\)
    🔗
    Difference law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }(f(x)-g(x))=\limit_{x \to a }f(x)-\limit_{x \to a }g(x)=L-M\)
    🔗
    Constant multiple law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }cf(x)=c\cdot \limit_{x \to a }f(x)=cL\)
    🔗
    Product law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }(f(x)\cdot g(x))=\limit_{x \to a }f(x)\cdot \limit_{x \to a }g(x)=L\cdot M\)
    🔗
    Quotient law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}=\frac{\limit_{x \to a }f(x)}{\limit_{x \to a }g(x)}=\frac{L}{M}\) for \(M\neq 0\)
    🔗
    Power law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }(f(x))^n=(\limit_{x \to a }f(x))^n=L^n\) for every positive integer \(n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Root law for limits: \(\limit_{x \to a }\sqrt[n]{f(x)}=\sqrt[n]{\limit_{x \to a }f(x)}=\sqrt[n]{L}\) for all \(L\) if \(n\) is odd and for \(L\gt 0\) if \(n\) is even. (If \(L=0\) then this cannot be computed with a two-sided limit).
    🔗
    🔗
    We now practice applying these limit laws to evaluate a limit.
    🔗

    Example 2.4.4. Evaluating a Limit Using Limit Laws.

    Use the limit laws to evaluate \(\limit_{x \to -3 }(4x+2).\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Let’s apply the limit laws one step at a time to be sure we understand how they work. We need to keep in mind the requirement that, at each application of a limit law, the new limits must exist for the limit law to be applied.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \limit_{x \to -3 }(4x+2) \amp=\limit_{x \to -3 }4x+\limit_{x \to -3 }2 \amp \amp\text{ Apply the sum law. }\\ \amp=4\cdot \limit_{x \to -3 }x+\limit_{x \to -3 }2 \amp \amp\text{ Apply the constant multiple law. }\\ \amp=\ds 4\cdot (-3)+2=-10. \amp \amp\text{ Apply the basic limit results and simplify. } \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.5. Using Limit Laws Repeatedly.

    Use the limit laws to evaluate \(\limit_{x \to 2 }\frac{2x^2-3x+1}{x^3+4}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    To find this limit, we need to apply the limit laws several times. Again, we need to keep in mind that as we rewrite the limit in terms of other limits, each new limit must exist for the limit law to be applied.
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \limit_{x \to 2 }\frac{2x^2-3x+1}{x^3+4}\amp=\frac{\limit_{x \to 2 }(2x^2-3x+1)}{\limit_{x \to 2 }(x^3+4)}\amp\text{ Apply the quotient law, making sure that. } (2)^3+4\neq 0\\ \amp=\frac{2\cdot \limit_{x \to 2 }x^2-3\cdot \limit_{x \to 2 }x+\limit_{x \to 2 }1}{\limit_{x \to 2 }x^3+\limit_{x \to 2 }4}\amp\text{ Apply the sum law and constant multiple law. }\\ \amp=\frac{2\cdot \limit_{x \to 2 }x^2-3\cdot \limit_{x \to 2 }x+\limit_{x \to 2 }1}{(\limit_{x \to 2 }x)^3+\limit_{x \to 2 }4}\amp\text{ Apply the power law. }\\ \amp=\frac{2(4)-3(2)+1}{(2)^3+4}=\frac{1}{4}.\amp\text{ Apply the basic limit laws and simplify. } \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.4.6.

    🔗
    Hint.
    Begin by applying the product law.
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.2 Limits of Polynomial and Rational Functions

    By now you have probably noticed that, in each of the previous examples, it has been the case that \(\limit_{x \to a }f(x)=f(a).\) This is not always true, but it does hold for all polynomials for any choice of \(a\) and for all rational functions at all values of \(a\) for which the rational function is defined.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.7.

    Let \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) be polynomial functions. Let \(a\) be a real number. Then,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to a }p(x)=p(a) \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to a }\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=\frac{p(a)}{q(a)} \text{ when } q(a)\neq 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Proof.

    To see that this theorem holds, consider the polynomial \(p(x)=c_nx^n+c_{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+c_1x+c_0.\) By applying the sum, constant multiple, and power laws, we end up with
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \limit_{x \to a }p(x) \amp=\limit_{x \to a }(c_nx^n+c^{n-1}x^{n-1}+\cdots+c_1x+c_0)\\ \amp=c_n(\limit_{x \to a }x)^n+c^{n-1}(\limit_{x \to a }x)^{n-1}+\cdots+c_1(\limit_{x \to a }x)+\limit_{x \to a }c_0\\ \amp=c_na^n+c^{n-1}a^{n-1}+\cdots+c_1a+c_0\\ \amp=p(a). \end{align*}
    It now follows from the quotient law that if \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are polynomials for which \(q(a)\neq 0,\) then
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to a }\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}=\frac{p(a)}{q(a)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗

    Example 2.4.8. Evaluating a Limit of a Rational Function.

    Evaluate the \(\limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{2x^2-3x+1}{5x+4}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Since 3 is in the domain of the rational function \(f(x)=\frac{2x^2-3x+1}{5x+4},\) we can calculate the limit by substituting 3 for \(x\) into the function. Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{2x^2-3x+1}{5x+4}=\frac{10}{19}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.4.9.

    Subsection 2.4.3 Additional Limit Evaluation Techniques

    As we have seen, we may evaluate easily the limits of polynomials and limits of some (but not all) rational functions by direct substitution. However, as we saw in the introductory section on limits, it is certainly possible for \(\limit_{x \to a }f(x)\) to exist when \(f(a)\) is undefined. The following observation allows us to evaluate many limits of this type:
    🔗
    If for all \(x\neq a,f(x)=g(x)\) over some open interval containing \(a\text{,}\) then \(\limit_{x \to a }f(x)=\limit_{x \to a }g(x).\)
    🔗
    To understand this idea better, consider the limit \(\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{x^2-1}{x-1}.\)
    🔗
    The function
    🔗
    \begin{align*} f(x)\amp=\frac{x^2-1}{x-1}\\ \amp=\frac{(x-1)}{(x+1)}{x-1} \end{align*}
    and the function \(g(x)=x+1\) are identical for all values of \(x\neq 1.\) The graphs of these two functions are shown in Figure 2.4.10.
    🔗
    We see that
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{x^2-1}{x-1}\amp=\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{(x-1)}{(x+1)}{x-1}\\ \amp=\limit_{x \to 1 }(x+1)\\ \amp=2. \end{align*}
    The limit has the form \(\limit_{x \to a }\frac{f(x)}{g(x)},\) where \(\limit_{x \to a }f(x)=0\) and \(\limit_{x \to a }g(x)=0.\) (In this case, we say that \(f(x)/g(x)\) has the indeterminate form \(0/0\text{ .) }\) The following problem-solving strategy provides a general outline for evaluating limits of this type.
    🔗

    Problem 2.4.11. Strategy For Calculating a Limit When \(f(x)/g(x)\) has the Indeterminate Form 0/0.

    1. First, we need to make sure that our function has the appropriate form and cannot be evaluated immediately using the limit laws.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We then need to find a function that is equal to \(h(x)=f(x)/g(x)\) for all \(x\neq a\) over some interval containing \(a\text{.}\) To do this, we may need to try one or more of the following steps:
      🔗
      1. If \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) are polynomials, we should factor each function and cancel out any common factors.
        🔗
        🔗
      2. If the numerator or denominator contains a difference involving a square root, we should try multiplying the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the expression involving the square root.
        🔗
        🔗
      3. If \(f(x)/g(x)\) is a complex fraction, we begin by simplifying it.
        🔗
        🔗
      🔗
    3. Last, we apply the limit laws.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    The next examples demonstrate the use of this Problem-Solving Strategy. Example 2.4.12 illustrates the factor-and-cancel technique; Example 2.4.14 shows multiplying by a conjugate. In Example 2.4.16, we look at simplifying a complex fraction.
    🔗

    Example 2.4.12. Evaluating a Limit by Factoring and Canceling.

    Evaluate \(\limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{x^2-3x}{2x^2-5x-3}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. The function \(f(x)=\frac{x^2-3x}{2x^2-5x-3}\) is undefined for \(x=3.\) In fact, if we substitute 3 into the function we get \(0/0,\) which is undefined. Factoring and canceling is a good strategy:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{x^2-3x}{2x^2-5x-3}=\limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{x(x-3)}{(x-3)}{(2x+1)} \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. For all \(x\neq 3,\frac{x^2-3x}{2x^2-5x-3}=\frac{x}{2x+1}.\) Therefore,
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{x(x-3)}{(x-3)}{(2x+1)}=\limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{x}{2x+1}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Evaluate using the limit laws:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to 3 }\frac{x}{2x+1}=\frac{3}{7}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.4.13.

    Example 2.4.14. Evaluating a Limit by Multiplying by a Conjugate.

    Evaluate \(\limit_{x \to -1 }\frac{\sqrt{x+2}-1}{x+1}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(\frac{\sqrt{x+2}-1}{x+1}\) has the form \(0/0\) at -1. Let’s begin by multiplying by \(\sqrt{x+2}+1,\) the conjugate of \(\sqrt{x+2}-1,\) on the numerator and denominator:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to -1 }\frac{\sqrt{x+2}-1}{x+1}=\limit_{x \to -1 }\frac{\sqrt{x+2}-1}{x+1}\cdot \frac{\sqrt{x+2}+1}{\sqrt{x+2}+1}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    2. We then multiply out the numerator. We don’t multiply out the denominator because we are hoping that the \((x+1)\) in the denominator cancels out in the end:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} =\limit_{x \to -1 }\frac{x+1}{(x+1)}{(\sqrt{x+2}+1)}. \end{equation*}
      Then we cancel:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} =\limit_{x \to -1 }\frac{1}{\sqrt{x+2}+1}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Last, we apply the limit laws:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to -1 }\frac{1}{\sqrt{x+2}+1}=\frac{1}{2}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.16. Evaluating a Limit by Simplifying a Complex Fraction.

    Evaluate \(\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{2}}{x-1}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    1. \(\ds\frac{\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{2}}{x-1}\) has the form \(0/0\) at 1.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. We simplify the algebraic fraction by multiplying by \(2(x+1)/2(x+1):\)
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{2}}{x-1}=\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{\frac{1}{x+1}-\frac{1}{2}}{x-1}\cdot \frac{2(x+1)}{2(x+1)}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
      Next, we multiply through the numerators. Do not multiply the denominators because we want to be able to cancel the factor \((x-1)\text{ : }\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} =\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{2-(x+1)}{2(x-1)}{(x+1)}. \end{equation*}
      Then, we simplify the numerator:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} =\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{-x+1}{2(x-1)(x+1)}. \end{equation*}
      Now we factor out -1 from the numerator:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} =\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{-(x-1)}{2(x-1)(x+1)}. \end{equation*}
      Then, we cancel the common factors of \((x-1)\text{ : }\)
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} =\limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{-1}{2(x+1)}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    3. Last, we evaluate using the limit laws:
      🔗
      \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to 1 }\frac{-1}{2(x+1)}=-\frac{1}{4}. \end{equation*}
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.18 does not fall neatly into any of the patterns established in the previous examples. However, with a little creativity, we can still use these same techniques.
    🔗

    Example 2.4.18. Evaluating a Limit When the Limit Laws Do Not Apply.

    Evaluate \(\limit_{x \to 0 }\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{5}{x(x-5)}\right).\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Both \(1/x\) and \(5/x(x-5)\) fail to have a limit at zero. Since neither of the two functions has a limit at zero, we cannot apply the sum law for limits; we must use a different strategy. In this case, we find the limit by performing addition and then applying one of our previous strategies. Observe that
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{x}+\frac{5}{x(x-5)}\amp=\frac{x-5+5}{x(x-5)}\\ \amp=\frac{x}{x(x-5)}. \end{align*}
    Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \limit_{x \to 0 }\left(\frac{1}{x}+\frac{5}{x(x-5)}\right)\amp=\limit_{x \to 0 }\frac{x}{x(x-5)}\\ \amp=\limit_{x \to 0 }\frac{1}{x-5}\\ \amp=-\frac{1}{5}. \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 2.4.19.

    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.4 One-Sided Limits

    Let’s now revisit one-sided limits. Simple modifications in the limit laws allow us to apply them to one-sided limits. For example, to apply the limit laws to a limit of the form \(\limit_{x\to a^-} h(x),\) we require the function \(h(x)\) to be defined over an open interval of the form \((b,a);\) for a limit of the form \(\limit_{x \to a^+} h(x),\) we require the function \(h(x)\) to be defined over an open interval of the form \((a,c).\) Example 2.4.20 illustrates this point.
    🔗

    Example 2.4.20. Evaluating a One-Sided Limit Using the Limit Laws.

    Evaluate each of the following limits, if possible.
    🔗
    1. \(\displaystyle \limit_{x \to 3^-}\sqrt{x-3}\)
      🔗
    2. \(\displaystyle \limit_{x \to 3^+}\sqrt{x-3}\)
      🔗
    Solution.
    Figure 2.4.21 illustrates the function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-3}\) and aids in our understanding of these limits.
    🔗
    1. The function \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-3}\) is defined over the interval \([3,+\infty).\) Since this function is not defined to the left of 3, we cannot apply the limit laws to compute \(\limit_{x \to 3^-}\sqrt{x-3}.\) In fact, since \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-3}\) is undefined to the left of 3, \(\limit_{x \to 3^-}\sqrt{x-3}\) does not exist.
      🔗
    2. Since \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-3}\) is defined to the right of 3, the limit laws do apply to \(\limit_{x \to 3^+}\sqrt{x-3}.\) By applying these limit laws we obtain \(\limit_{x \to 3^+}\sqrt{x-3}=0.\)
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.5 The Squeeze Theorem

    The techniques we have developed thus far work very well for algebraic functions, but we are still unable to evaluate limits of very basic trigonometric functions. The next theorem, called the squeeze theorem, proves very useful for establishing basic trigonometric limits. This theorem allows us to calculate limits by “squeezing” a function, with a limit at a point \(a\) that is unknown, between two functions having a common known limit at \(a\text{.}\) Figure 2.4.25 illustrates this idea.
    🔗

    Theorem 2.4.26. The Squeeze Theorem.

    Let \(f(x),g(x),\) and \(h(x)\) be defined for all \(x\neq a\) over an open interval containing \(a\text{.}\) If
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} f(x)\leq g(x)\leq h(x) \end{equation*}
    for all \(x\neq a\) in an open interval containing \(a\) and
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{x \to a }f(x)=L=\limit_{x \to a }h(x) \end{equation*}
    where \(L\) is a real number, then \(\limit_{x \to a }g(x)=L.\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.

    For \(\varepsilon\gt 0\) there exist \(\delta_1\gt 0\text{ and }\delta_2\gt 0\) such that \(|f(x)-L|\lt \varepsilon\) whenever \(0\lt|x-a|\lt \delta_1\) and \(|h(x)-L|\lt \varepsilon\) whenever \(0\lt|x-a|\lt \delta_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\delta\) be the smaller of \(\delta_1\text{ and }\delta_2\text{.}\) For any \(x\) satisfying \(0\lt|x-a|\lt \delta\text{,}\) then \(|f(x)-L|\lt \varepsilon\) (or \(-\varepsilon\lt f(x)-L\lt \varepsilon\)) and \(|h(x)-L|\lt \varepsilon\) (or \(-\varepsilon\lt h(x)-L\lt \varepsilon\)).
    🔗
    Putting it all together,
    \begin{align*} f(x)\le g(x)\amp\le h(x) \\ f(x)-L\le g(x)-L\amp\le h(x)-L \\ -\varepsilon\lt f(x)-L \le g(x)-L\amp\le h(x)-L\lt \varepsilon \\ -\varepsilon\lt g(x)-L\amp\lt \varepsilon \\ |g(x)-L| \amp\lt \varepsilon \end{align*}
    Therefore, \(\limit_{x\to a}g(x)=L\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 2.4.27. Applying the Squeeze Theorem.

    Apply the squeeze theorem to evaluate \(\limit_{x \to 0 }x\cos x.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    Because \(-1\leq \cos x\leq 1\) for all \(x\text{,}\) we have \(-|x|\leq x\cos x\leq |x|\text{.}\) Since \(\limit_{x \to 0 }(-|x|)=0=\limit_{x \to 0 }|x|,\) from the squeeze theorem, we obtain \(\limit_{x \to 0 }x\cos x=0.\) The graphs of \(f(x)=-|x|,g(x)=x\cos x,\) and \(h(x)=|x|\) are shown in Figure 2.4.28.

    Subsection 2.4.6 A Few Important Trigonometric Limits

    We now use the squeeze theorem to tackle several very important limits. Although this discussion is somewhat lengthy, these limits prove invaluable for the development of the material in both the next section and the next chapter. The first of these limits is \(\limit_{\theta \to 0}\sin \theta.\) Consider the unit circle shown in Figure 2.4.30. In the figure, we see that \(\sin \theta\) is the \(y\)-coordinate on the unit circle and it corresponds to the line segment shown in blue. The radian measure of angle \(\theta\) is the length of the arc it subtends on the unit circle. Therefore, we see that for \(0\lt \theta\lt \frac{\pi}{2},0\lt \sin \theta\lt \theta.\)
    🔗
    Because \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^+}0=0\) and \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^+}\theta=0,\) by using the squeeze theorem we conclude that
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{\theta\to 0^+}\sin \theta=0. \end{equation*}
    To see that \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^-}\sin \theta=0\) as well, observe that for \(-\frac{\pi }{2}\lt \theta\lt 0,0\lt -\theta\lt \frac{\pi }{2}\) and hence, \(0\lt \sin (-\theta)\lt -\theta.\) Consequently, \(0\lt -\sin \theta\lt -\theta.\) It follows that \(0\gt \sin \theta\gt \theta.\) An application of the squeeze theorem produces the desired limit. Thus, since \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^+}\sin \theta=0\) and \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^-}\sin \theta=0,\)
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \limit_{\theta\to0}\sin \theta=0.\tag{2.4.1} \end{equation}
    Next, using the identity \(\cos \theta=\sqrt{1-\sin^2>\theta}\) for \(-\frac{\pi }{2}\lt \theta\lt \frac{\pi }{2},\) we see that
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \limit_{\theta\to0}\cos \theta=\limit_{\theta\to0}\sqrt{1-\sin^2>\theta}=1.\tag{2.4.2} \end{equation}
    We now take a look at a limit that plays an important role in later chapters—namely, \(\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{\sin \theta}{\theta}.\) To evaluate this limit, we use the unit circle in Figure 2.4.31. Notice that this figure adds one additional triangle to Figure 2.4.31. We see that the length of the side opposite angle \(\theta \) in this new triangle is \(\tan\theta.\) Thus, we see that for \(0\lt \theta\lt \frac{\pi}{2},\sin \theta\lt \theta\lt \tan \theta.\)
    🔗
    By dividing by \(\sin \theta\) in all parts of the inequality, we obtain
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1\lt \frac{\theta}{\sin \theta}\lt \frac{1}{\cos \theta}. \end{equation*}
    Equivalently, we have
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} 1\gt \frac{\sin \theta}{\theta}\gt \cos \theta. \end{equation*}
    Since \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^+}1=1=\limit_{\theta\to 0^+}\cos \theta,\) we conclude that \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^+}\frac{\sin \theta}{\theta}=1.\) By applying a manipulation similar to that used in demonstrating that \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^-}\sin \theta=0,\) we can show that \(\limit_{\theta\to 0^-}\frac{\sin \theta}{\theta}=1.\) Thus,
    🔗
    \begin{equation} \limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{\sin \theta}{\theta}=1.\tag{2.4.3} \end{equation}
    In Example 2.4.32 we use this limit to establish \(\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{1-\cos \theta}{\theta}=0.\) This limit also proves useful in later chapters.
    🔗

    Example 2.4.32. Evaluating an Important Trigonometric Limit.

    Evaluate \(\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{1-\cos \theta}{\theta}.\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    In the first step, we multiply by the conjugate so that we can use a trigonometric identity to convert the cosine in the numerator to a sine:
    🔗
    \begin{align*} \limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{1-\cos \theta}{\theta}\amp=\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{1-\cos \theta}{\theta}\cdot \frac{1+\cos \theta}{1+\cos \theta}\\ \amp=\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{1-\cos^2\theta}{\theta(1+\cos \theta)}\\ \amp=\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{\sin^2 \theta}{\theta(1+\cos \theta)}\\ \amp=\limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{\sin \theta}{\theta}\cdot \frac{\sin \theta}{1+\cos \theta}\\ \amp=1\cdot \frac{0}{2}=0. \end{align*}
    Therefore,
    🔗
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{\theta\to0}\frac{1-\cos \theta}{\theta}=0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Subsection 2.4.7 Proofs of the Limit Laws (Optional)

    We now prove Theorem 2.4.3
    🔗

    Proof.

    To start, we need the triangle inequality, which says that for any real numbers \(x\text{ and }y\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} |x+y|\lt |x|+|y| \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Sum law for limits: For \(\varepsilon\gt 0\text{.}\) Since the limit for \(f(x)\) exists, then there exists \(\delta_1\gt 0\) such that when \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta_1\) then \(|f(x)-L|\lt \ds\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Also, since the limit for \(g(x)\) exists, there is some \(\delta_2\gt 0\) such that when \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta_1\) then \(|g(x)-M|\lt \ds\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\delta\) be the smaller of \(\delta_1\text{ and }\delta_2\) (so \(\delta\le \delta_1\) and \(\delta\le\delta_2\)). Whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta\text{,}\) then using the Triangle Inequality,
    \begin{align*} |\left(f(x)+g(x)\right)-\left(L+M\right)| \amp =|\left(f(x)-L\right)+\left(g(x)-M\right)|\\ \amp \le|f(x)-L|+|g(x)-M|\\ \amp \lt \frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\\ \amp =\varepsilon \end{align*}
    🔗
    Difference law for limits: Choose \(\delta\) the same way as in the sum law for limits.
    🔗
    Again using the Triangle Inequality, whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta\text{,}\) then
    \begin{align*} |\left(f(x)-g(x)\right)-\left(L-M\right)| \amp =|\left(f(x)-L\right)-\left(g(x)-M\right)|\\ \amp =|(f(x)-L)+\left(-(g(x)-M)\right)|\\ \amp \lt|f(x)-L|+|-\left(g(x)-M\right)|\\ \amp =|f(x)-L|+|g(x)-M|\\ \amp \lt \frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\\ \amp =\varepsilon \end{align*}
    🔗
    Constant multiple law for limits: If \(c=0\) then this is just a case of Theorem 2.4.1
    🔗
    If \(c\ne 0\text{,}\) let \(\varepsilon\gt 0\text{.}\) Since the limit for \(f(x)\) exists, there is some \(\delta\gt 0\) such that \(|f(x)-L|\lt \ds\frac{\varepsilon}{|c|}\) whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta\text{.}\) For such \(x\text{,}\)
    \begin{align*} |cf(x)-cL|\amp =|c||f(x)-L|\\ \amp \lt |c|\cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{|c|}\\ \amp =\varepsilon \end{align*}
    🔗
    Product law for limits: Let \(\varepsilon\gt 0\text{.}\) First, consider the case if \(L=0\text{ or }M=0\)Since the limits for \(f(x)\text{ and }g(x)\) exist, there exists \(\delta_1\gt 0\) and \(\delta_2\gt 0\) such that \(|f(x)-L|\lt \sqrt{\varepsilon}\) when \(|x-a|\lt \delta_1\) and \(|g(x)-M|\lt \sqrt{\varepsilon}\) when \(|x-a|\lt \delta_2\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let \(\delta\) be the smaller of \(\delta_1\text{ and }\delta_2\) like we did in the sum law for limits. For any \(|x-a|\lt \delta\text{,}\)
    \begin{align*} |f(x)g(x)-LM| \amp =|f(x)g(x)-0|\\ \amp =|f(x)g(x)|\\ \amp =|f(x)||g(x)|\\ \amp \lt\sqrt{\varepsilon}\sqrt{\varepsilon}\\ \amp =\varepsilon \end{align*}
    🔗
    Now, for the more general case, assume \(L\ne 0\text{ and }M\ne 0\text{.}\) Since the limit for \(g(x)\) exists, then there is some \(\delta_1\gt 0\) such that whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta_1\text{,}\) then
    \begin{align*} |g(x)-M|\amp\lt \varepsilon \\ M-\varepsilon\lt \amp g(x)\lt M+\varepsilon \\ |g(x)| \amp |M|+\varepsilon \end{align*}
    (the last line is true regardless of whether \(M\) is positive or negative)
    🔗
    Since the limit for \(f(x)\) exists, there is some \(\delta_2\gt 0\) such that whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta_2\text{,}\) then \(|f(x)-L|\lt\ds\frac{\varepsilon}{2(|M|+\varepsilon)}\text{.}\) Also, there is some \(\delta_3\gt 0\) such that \(|g(x)-M|\lt \ds\frac{\varepsilon}{2|L|}\) whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta_3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s put the pieces altogether by letting \(\delta\) be the minimum of \(\delta_1,\delta_2,\text{ and }\delta_3\text{.}\) For any x satisfying \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta\text{,}\) again use the Triangle Inequality to show
    \begin{align*} |f(x)g(x)-LM| \amp =|f(x)g(x)+0-LM|\\ \amp =|f(x)g(x)-Lg(x)+Lg(x)-LM|\\ \amp \le |f(x)-Lg(x)|+|Lg(x)-LM|\\ \amp =|g(x)||f(x)-L|+|L||g(x)-M|\\ \amp \lt (|M|+\varepsilon)\frac{\varepsilon}{2(|M|+\varepsilon)}+|L|\frac{\varepsilon}{2|L|}\\ \amp =\frac{\varepsilon}{2}+\frac{\varepsilon}{2}\\ \amp =\varepsilon \end{align*}
    🔗
    Quotient law for limits: This limit law will follow quickly if we first show that \(\limit_{x\to a}\frac{1}{g(x)}=\frac{1}{M}\text{:}\) Assume for simplicity that \(M\gt 0\text{.}\) Since the limit for \(g(x)\) exists, then given \(\varepsilon\gt 0\) there exists \(\delta\gt 0\) such that \(\ds|g(x)-M|\lt \frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon}\) whenever \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta\text{.}\) Note that
    \begin{equation*} \ds 0\lt\frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon}=M-\frac{M}{1+M\varepsilon}\lt M\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Following the inequalities down the rabbit hole, for any \(x\) satisfying \(0\lt |x-a|\lt \delta\text{,}\)
    \begin{align*} |g(x)-M| \amp \lt \frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon}\\ -\frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon}\lt \amp g(x)-M\lt \frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon} \\ M-\frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon}\lt \amp g(x)-M\lt M+\frac{M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon} \\ \frac{M}{1+M\varepsilon}\lt \amp g(x)\lt \frac{M+2M^2\varepsilon}{1+M\varepsilon} \\ \frac{1+M\varepsilon}{M+2M^2\varepsilon}\amp \lt \frac{1}{g(x)}\lt \frac{1+M\varepsilon}{M} \\ \frac{1+M\varepsilon}{M+2M^2\varepsilon}-\frac{1}{M}\amp \lt \frac{1}{g(x)}-\frac{1}{M}\lt \frac{1+M\varepsilon}{M}-\frac{1}{M}\\ -\frac{\varepsilon}{1+2M\varepsilon} \amp\lt \frac{1}{g(x)}-\frac{1}{M}\lt \varepsilon \end{align*}
    Since \(\ds\frac{\varepsilon}{1+2M\varepsilon}\lt\varepsilon \text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \left|\frac{1}{g(x)}-\frac{1}{M}\right|\lt\varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Now, if \(M\lt 0\text{,}\) define \(f(x)=-g(x)\text{.}\) The constant multiple law of limits shows that \(\ds\lim_{x\to a}\frac{1}{f(x)}=-\frac{1}{M}\gt 0\) and so \(\ds\lim_{x\to a}\frac{1}{g(x)}=\frac{1}{M}\)
    🔗
    Finally, to prove the quotient law for limits, rewrite \(\ds\lim_{x\to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) as \(\limit_{x\to a}\left(f(x)\cdot\frac{1}{g(x)}\right)\) and apply the product law for limits.
    🔗
    Power law for limits: This is a repeated application of the product law for limits
    🔗
    Root law for limits: First start with the case when \(L=1\text{.}\) Let \(\varepsilon\gt 0\text{.}\) Without loss of generality, assume \(\varepsilon\lt 1\text{.}\) Then there exists \(\delta\gt 0\) such that \(|f(x)-1|\lt \varepsilon\) whenever \(0\lt|x-a|\lt \delta\) (which makes \(f(x)\gt 0\)). It can be verified that
    \begin{align*} f(x)-1 \amp =\left(\sqrt[n]{f(x)}-1\right)\left(\left(\sqrt[n]{f(x)}\right)^{n-1}+\left(\sqrt[n]{f(x)}\right)^{n-2}\right.\\ \amp\left.+\cdots+\left(\sqrt[n]{f(x)}\right)+1\right) \end{align*}
    So,
    \begin{align*} \left|\sqrt[n]{f(x)}-1\right|\amp=\left|\frac{f(x)-1}{1+f(x)^{1/n}+f(x)^{2/n}+\cdots+f(x)^{(n-1)/n}}\right| \\ \amp \lt |f(x)-1|\\ \amp \lt \varepsilon \end{align*}
    Therefore, if \(\limit_{x\to a}f(x)=1\) then \(\limit_{x\to a}\sqrt[n]{f(x)}=1.\)
    🔗
    Now suppose \(\limit_{x\to a}f(x)=L\) where \(L\) is any nonzero number. Define \(\ds g(x)=\frac{1}{L}f(x)\text{.}\) Using the constant multiple law for limits, \(\limit_{x\to a}g(x)=1\text{.}\) Thus, by the work above, \(\limit_{x\to a}\sqrt[n]{g(x)}=1\text{.}\) Again using the constant multiple law for limits,
    \begin{equation*} \limit_{x\to a}\sqrt[n]{f(x)}=\limit_{x\to a}\sqrt[n]{L\cdot g(x)}=\limit_{x\to a}\sqrt[n]{L}\sqrt[n]{g(x)}=\sqrt[n]{L}\cdot 1=\sqrt[n]{L} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Finally, suppose \(L=0\) with \(n\) odd. It can be shown that whenever \(|f(x)-0|\lt\varepsilon^n\text{,}\) then \(|\sqrt[n]{f(x)}-0|\lt \varepsilon\text{.}\)
